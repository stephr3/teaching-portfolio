Teaching Philosophy

As an English teacher, I see myself not as a provider of knowledge, but as a facilitator – one who helps students activate their own background knowledge and build up new connections and ideas. I hope that my students can learn not only to rely on me, but also to rely on themselves and their classmates to learn new concepts. I try to achieve this by creating a comfortable, interactive classroom environment where we study relevant topics and language. I encourage my students to communicate their own needs, expectations, and goals to me so that I can better facilitate their learning. I can then utilize a variety of methods and approaches based on my individual classes and student needs assessments.

One approach I have found to be useful is the social constructivist approach. This approach looks at language both as a tool to connect with others and as a vessel of culture, and  it emphasizes connection and cooperation among students (Beck & Kosnik, 2006). To apply this in the classroom, I plan group work and task-based activities to utilize students' unique backgrounds and knowledge. For example, my students might be "experts for a day", and share information they know about their hometown, chosen profession, or hobbies with the class. Students might even do their own research to present a poster session to other teachers and students at their language institute.

I also use a task-based learning approach in my classroom. Students are asked to do real-life and useful tasks in the classroom, and language and structures are organically taught based on what is needed to complete the specified task (Larsen-Freeman, 1985).  One activity I have done is have students find a learning resource at their school and teach their classmates how to get there. A higher level group of students might publish a newsletter to share with other classes. Task-based learning can be combined with systematic vocabulary and grammar teaching to give students successful experience with everyday tasks that they may be faced with again outside the classroom.

Another important aspect to my classroom is feedback and error correction, which may change depending on lesson objectives for the day. For example, if perfect production is the goal, I give outright and immediate error correction by repeating back a phrase or sentence correctly or asking students leading questions to fix their errors. On the other hand, if fluency is the goal for the day, I will instead focus on breakdowns in communication or errors in usage and meaning. One practice I've found useful during fluency activities is to take notes on common accuracy mistakes that I hear during a class activity. I can then focus the class on correcting these errors together after the activity is finished, so they can reflect on their accuracy as well as conversational fluency.

By using multiple approaches to guide my class planning and staying flexible enough to respond to students' needs, I hope to create a comfortable and dynamic classroom environment. Part of the joy of teaching is that no class is ever the same, and I can constantly learn from student assessment and feedback which methods and approaches work best for a particular group of students. I look forward to continuing to grow and adjusting my teaching philosophy based on the daily lessons I learn from my students, colleagues, and research in the field.
